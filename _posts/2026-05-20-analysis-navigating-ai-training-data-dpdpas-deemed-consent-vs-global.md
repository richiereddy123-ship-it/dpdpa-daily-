The rapid evolution of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has brought the critical issue of training data under intense regulatory scrutiny. For businesses operating in India and globally, understanding the lawful bases for processing personal data for AI model development is paramount. India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDPA), while establishing a comprehensive data protection framework, presents a distinct approach compared to the more established General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and specific guidance from bodies like the UK Information Commissioner's Office (ICO). This analysis anchors on the DPDPA, comparing its provisions for AI training data against these global counterparts.

## DPDPA's Deemed Consent: A Defined but Ambiguous Path for AI
The DPDPA primarily relies on the explicit consent of the Data Principal for the lawful processing of personal data, as outlined in Section 4. However, recognising the practical limitations of explicit consent for all processing activities, the Act introduces the concept of "deemed consent" in Section 7. This allows for processing without explicit consent under specific circumstances, such as when the Data Principal has voluntarily provided their personal data and it is "necessary for purposes for which the Data Principal has voluntarily provided their personal data" (Section 7(a)). Other grounds include processing for public interest (Section 7(e)) or for purposes "as may be prescribed" by the Central Government (Section 7(d)).

For AI training, the reliance on deemed consent, particularly Section 7(a), presents both opportunities and interpretational challenges. The core question is whether training an AI model falls within the "reasonable expectation" of the Data Principal when they voluntarily provide their data. For instance, if a user provides data to improve a service, is using that data to train an underlying AI model for that service reasonably expected? The DPDPA currently lacks specific guidance on this, creating a potential gap for large-scale, diverse AI training datasets. Unlike GDPR, the DPDPA does not include a general "legitimate interest" ground for processing, which could be seen as a stricter approach, limiting the flexibility of Data Fiduciaries. Furthermore, Data Principals retain rights such as the right to erasure (Section 13) and grievance redressal (Section 14), which could impact the integrity and availability of AI training datasets.

## GDPR's Legitimate Interest: Flexibility with Accountability
In contrast, the GDPR offers a broader lawful basis for processing personal data, including "legitimate interest" under Article 6(1)(f). This ground permits processing if it is "necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or by a third party, except where such interests are overridden by the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject." This framework is often leveraged for AI training, as obtaining explicit consent for vast, constantly evolving datasets can be impractical.

To rely on legitimate interest, controllers must conduct a three-part test: identify a legitimate interest, demonstrate that the processing is necessary for that interest, and balance the controller's interests against the rights and freedoms of the data subjects. This balancing test requires a robust assessment of potential impacts on data subjects, including transparency, data minimisation, and the availability of opt-out mechanisms. While offering significant flexibility, GDPR's legitimate interest ground demands high accountability and thorough risk assessments, often necessitating Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) to identify and mitigate risks associated with AI training.

## UK ICO Guidance: Operationalising Legitimate Interest for AI
The UK ICO has provided extensive guidance on applying data protection principles, particularly legitimate interest, to AI development. This guidance elaborates on how organisations can responsibly use personal data for AI training under Article 6(1)(f) of the UK GDPR. The ICO emphasises that even when relying on legitimate interest, the processing must be fair, transparent, and proportionate. It stresses the importance of considering whether data subjects would "reasonably expect" their data to be used for AI training, aligning with a similar underlying principle in DPDPA's deemed consent, but with a different legal basis and framework for assessment.

The ICO guidance advises controllers to conduct comprehensive legitimate interest assessments (LIAs), detailing the benefits of AI development, the necessity of using personal data, and the measures taken to protect data subjects' rights. It also highlights that publicly available data is not automatically fair game for AI training; a lawful basis, such as legitimate interest, is still required, along with adherence to other data protection principles like data minimisation and security. This detailed, practical guidance provides a clear roadmap for organisations developing AI, something currently less explicit in the DPDPA.

## Comparative Analysis: Stricter, Looser, or Silent?
Comparing these frameworks, the DPDPA's reliance on "deemed consent" (Section 7) for AI training can be seen as both potentially *stricter* and *looser* depending on interpretation. It is *stricter* in that it does not offer a broad "legitimate interest" ground like GDPR, requiring Data Fiduciaries to fit AI training into specific, enumerated deemed consent categories. This limits the flexibility seen in European jurisdictions. However, it could be *looser* if the "reasonable expectation" clause within Section 7(a) is interpreted broadly without specific regulatory guidance, potentially allowing for processing that might not withstand a rigorous GDPR balancing test.

Conversely, GDPR's Article 6(1)(f) offers greater *flexibility* for AI training but imposes a *stricter* burden of proof and accountability through the legitimate interest assessment and balancing test. The UK ICO guidance further *tightens* this by providing detailed requirements for fairness, transparency, and proportionality specifically for AI. The DPDPA is largely *silent* on specific considerations for AI training data, leaving Data Fiduciaries to extrapolate from general principles. While sector-specific regulations, such as those from the RBI, and IT Rules may impose additional data handling requirements, the DPDPA remains the primary privacy law governing personal data processing for AI.

## Practical Takeaway
For Indian businesses, General Counsels, and Data Protection Officers engaged in AI development, the current landscape necessitates a cautious and proactive approach. Given the DPDPA's reliance on deemed consent and the absence of specific AI-centric guidance, it is crucial to: (1) clearly define the "purpose" for which data is collected and ensure AI training aligns with the Data Principal's "reasonable expectation" under Section 7(a); (2) explore if AI training could fall under "public interest" (Section 7(e)) or future "prescribed purposes" (Section 7(d)), while awaiting specific rules; (3) implement robust consent mechanisms for situations where deemed consent is insufficient; (4) conduct internal assessments akin to DPIAs and LIAs, even if not explicitly mandated, to demonstrate accountability and mitigate risks; and (5) stay abreast of forthcoming DPDPA rules, advisories, and judicial interpretations that may clarify the application of deemed consent to AI training data.
